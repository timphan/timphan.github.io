by Dick Allen

<p>
Stumps. Railroad tracks. Early sicknesses, <br />
the blue one, especially.<br />
Your first love rounding a corner,<br />
that snowy minefield.<br />
</p>

<p>
Whether you step lightly or heavily,<br />
you have to get over to that tree line a hundred yards in the distance<br />
before evening falls,<br />
letting no one see you wend your way,<br />
</p>

<p>
that wonderful, old-fashioned word, wend,<br />
meaning “to proceed, to journey,<br />
to travel from one place to another,”<br />
as from bed to breakfast, breakfast to imbecile work.<br />
</p>

<p>
You have to get over your resentments,<br />
the sun in the morning and the moon at night,<br />
all those shadows of yourself you left behind<br />
on odd little tables.<br />
</p>

<p>
Tote that barge! Lift that bale! You have to<br />
cross that river, jump that hedge, surmount that slogan,<br />
crawl over this ego or that eros,<br />
then hoist yourself up onto that yonder mountain.<br />
</p>

<p>
Another old-fashioned word, yonder, meaning <br />
“that indicated place, somewhere generally seen <br />
or just beyond sight.” If you would recover, <br />
you have to get over the shattered autos in the backwoods lot<br />
</p>

<p>
to that bridge in the darkness <br />
where the sentinels stand <br />
guarding the border with their half-slung rifles, <br />
warned of the likes of you. <br />
</p>
