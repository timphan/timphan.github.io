System Failure

Jan 1 0001

There’s a picture of a toddler washed up on a Turkish beach. He’s a Syrian boy who died with several others, trying to escape the war. A life ended before it even had a chance.

The desperation of people trying to escape a war they wanted no part in, that’s just human nature. What wouldn’t you do, to give your family a better future? Even while knowing the risks of the perilous journey, they leave in droves, clammoring for the perceived safety of European lands. They are being rejected, shunned, beaten, threatened.

European leaders are convening to discuss the escalated crisis. They are blaming the traffickers, they bicker about how many people to let through their gates, they are skewing towards nativist right-wing politics that wants a purer (less Arab/Muslim) Europe.

These things are important, and have vastly significant socio and political consequences for the societies of Europe. But maybe you’re missing the forest for the trees, these things are symptons of the festering Mideast crisis that’s been spinning out of control for years. It started as an anti al-Assad protest, which has since spiraled into an insane clusterfuck of diverse militant groups all vying for power. I’m not sure the Western countries know who to support, there can be no true winner in this disaster. Only the losers, like that toddler, and all the others who won’t be photographed.

This is our faults. Policy failure is not giving this boy a chance. I don’t mean that we should invade the Mideast to kill ISIS in 90 days. Even then, you’d still need to design policies and governance that gives people a chance at both stability and opportunity. Ending the war isn’t winning the peace, and that’s what we’ve all got to figure out. What does a peaceful, inclusive, and hopeful society look like? I can’t answer that question for the Syrians, for the Europeans, for that will be their battle.
